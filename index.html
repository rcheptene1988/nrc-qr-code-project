Host & Guest Speakers

Barra Best,

Host

An accomplished presenter and journalist, Barra Best has been broadcasting across the airwaves for BBC Northern Ireland for more than 15 years.


After cutting his teeth as a news and current affairs reporter Barra moved to the weather team in 2010 and remains there today, forecasting the weather and reporting on it when weather becomes the news story.


He has also fronted a number of popular BBC 1 series including ‘Walk The Line’, which charted the lost railways of Northern Ireland, ‘Coast Lives’ which examined the lives of those that live, work, and holiday around our coast, and ‘Barra’s Return of The Wild’ which explored some of our threatened native species and the conservation efforts to bring them back from the brink of extinction.

Topic:

An Education of Hope: why young people are succeeding on their terms

Martin
Illingworth,

Senior Lecturer in Education, Sheffield Hallam University

Martin Illingworth is Senior Lecturer in Education at Sheffield Hallam University, consultant teacher with The National Association for the Teaching of English and Associate Speaker with Independent Thinking.


Martin is a leading voice in education today. He brings with him good humour, common sense and a passionate belief that what your students need is an education that offers them hope. Martin will inspire you to think about the education you offer.


Martin is an English specialist with twenty-four years of teaching experience, both as classroom teacher and as Faculty leader. At Sheffield Hallam, he has responsibility for training English teachers on Secondary English, Drama and Music PGCEs. He has conducted research in Toronto, Canada, into teacher training abroad. He collated his findings into a short book ‘Education in the age of the information superhighway’ (2011) and was also published in The Canadian Journal for Education.


In his book, ‘Think Before You Teach’ (2015), Martin asks teachers to reflect on why and how they intend to teach. ‘An education of hope’ is the offer that Martin extends, in inviting teachers to think about taking responsibility for what happens in their own classrooms.


Martin works with schools and universities throughout the UK including appearances at The University of Nottingham, The Harris Academy in South London and at The National Primary Grammar Conference in Oxford (with David Crystal and Ronald Carter), The National Festival of Education, The National Education Show of Wales and King’s College, London.


Published in March 2020, Martin’s latest book ‘Forget School’ looks at the ways in which young people are meeting the needs of the new digital age as they face the world of work and the ways in which schools need to step up in their efforts to support their pupils. Martin’s forthcoming book is entitled ‘Confidence’ and considers how teachers can develop their own confidence and promote it in their students in these uncertain times.

Topic:

Does active learning need active partnership?

Dr Kay Hack
(PFHEA)

Lead Consultant (Education) for Advance-HE

Kay Hack leads a team of senior consultants to deliver a range of services to the Higher Education sector to support strategic leadership and change in Higher Education Institutions. She led the revision of the Advance HE Student Success Framework Series supporting the sector to address the key themes aimed at improving student engagement, experience, and outcomes. This work highlighted the importance of listening to student voices in the development of policy and practice.


Dr Kay Hack (PFHEA) will invite delegates to consider the value of engaging students as active partners in co-creating their learning experiences. Co-creation approaches can foster an authentic, inclusive, and current curriculum, motivating students to engage, gain skills, and build confidence, critical building blocks for active learning.


The benefits extend to staff and institutions, introducing new perspectives and insights, and ensuring inclusivity and student-centeredness. Opportunities for co-creation range from developing course materials to designing governance processes and campus spaces. However, authentic, and ethical co-creation processes are challenging. Co-creation extends beyond seeking feedback on pre-made decisions and must be handled carefully to avoid exploitation or exclusion. For an ethical, equitable, and sustainable approach to co-creation, it needs to be embedded within the core practices of the college; enabling all students to benefit.
